The Messy Side of Digital Forensics


The first ever major cyber crime was committed on November 2nd 1988, when a computer-worm was released onto the internet from a computer belonging to the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Within 24 hours, it had infected 10% of the entire internet and caused an estimated $100,000 in damage. As a result, the U.S. Government gave graduate student Robert Morris the official title, First Ever Individual to be Convicted of a Cyber Crime.

With the emergence of a new type of criminal comes the birth of a new field of study. Digital forensics is the process of collecting, interpreting, and preserving electronic data to be presented in a court of law. It requires analyzing data from computers, phones, and servers to understand and prevent cyber crime. The goal of digital forensics is to draw an accurate picture of the events that occurred on a digital device.

However, this is rarely as easy as downloading a device's hard drive and searching for malware. In the classroom, we learn about how to make sure everything goes right. Evidence has a perfect chain of custody, we work from full physical images, and evidence is easy to verify. But what happens when things don't go right? How to you forensically preserve an Instagram account? What if your data source is a 100TB SAN? How do we defend new and emerging practices on the stand?

Our guest speaker Jeff Best will be sharing some of his experiences working in the field and courts of digital forensics. Jeff Best is the current Senior Vice President of Cyber Product Management for Citibank.

Updates in the World of Cyber


A Chinese 'spy balloon' has been spotted floating over American soil! A resident of Billings, Montana took a recording of a ballon the size of 'three buses' floating over a lake and posted it on social media February 1st.

Over the past couple days the posting has generated a lot of attention, especially among United States officials. U.S. House Speaker Kevin McCarthy replied to the incident on social media saying: "China's brazen disregard for U.S. sovereignty is a destabilizing action that must be addressed, and President Biden cannot be silent."

China responded that the claims are based only on speculation, and do not claim ownership of the balloon. A second balloon has recently been spotted in Canada.
